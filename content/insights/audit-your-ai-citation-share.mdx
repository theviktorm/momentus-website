Most CMOs can quote their organic share of voice down to the decimal. Ask them what percentage of category queries on ChatGPT, Perplexity or Gemini cite them as a default brand, and you get a polite shrug. That gap is no longer affordable. AI referral traffic is up 123% year-over-year. ChatGPT's visitor volume alone is up roughly 8x in the last twelve months. Pricing pages now get hit by humans who already heard the verdict from a model — and the verdict is rarely "all options are equal." If you don't know your citation share, you are paying for clicks against a recommendation engine you've never read.

## What "citation share" actually means

Citation share is not one number. It's the per-platform answer to a single question: when a prospect in your category asks an AI system the question that should lead to you, are you named, named first, named with the right context, or invisible? The mechanics differ enough across the four major surfaces that you have to grade them separately.

ChatGPT, when grounded in search, cites — meaning it names sources and brands inline. The citation isn't always a link; sometimes you're a phrase. Perplexity goes further: it cites *and* ranks, sources are numbered, the order is meaningful, and the same query can return different orderings depending on which retrieval mode is on. Gemini summarizes — it often gives an opinionated answer with brand names embedded in prose, but with weaker source attribution. Google's AI Overview blends — it weaves a top-of-page generative answer over the classic blue links, and you can be in one surface and absent from the other on the same SERP.

The point isn't to memorize the mechanics. The point is that "are we showing up in AI" is the wrong question. The right one is: are we showing up *the way we want to* on each surface, for each high-intent query type, in our category. That's what the next 20 minutes is for.

## The 5-query audit

Pick five queries — one of each type below — and run them on ChatGPT (logged out, fresh session), Perplexity (default mode), and Gemini. Open three browsers or three private windows, tile them. The 45 minutes the math suggests collapses to about 20 if you parallelize. Take a screenshot of every result. You will use them.

### 1. Category-defining

**Template:** `best [category] in [region] 2026`
Example: *best B2B fintech tools for European startups 2026*.

**What to look for:** the list it generates without prompting, in the order it generates it. The first three names are doing most of the work — that's where the recommendation actually lives.

**Rubric:** are you in the list? Where? Are the brands above you actually competitors, or category-adjacent companies that happen to have better content infrastructure?

### 2. Comparison

**Template:** `[your brand] vs [top competitor]`

**What to look for:** the framing. Models don't just compare specs — they assign roles. "X is for enterprise, Y is for fast-moving teams." Whatever role they give you will get repeated for months. If the framing is wrong now, it's getting baked in.

**Rubric:** does the comparison name the *right* axes for your category? Does it cite primary sources (your site, your customer pages, third-party reviews) or secondary ones (a 2023 listicle on an SEO farm)?

### 3. Recommendation

**Template:** `who should I pick for [job-to-be-done]`
Example: *who should I pick for headless commerce on a $50k/mo GMV store*.

**What to look for:** specificity. A model that knows your category will give specific guidance. A model that doesn't will hedge. If every result is "it depends, consider X, Y, Z" without ranking, you have a generic-category problem, not a brand problem.

**Rubric:** is there a clear top recommendation? Is it you? If not, is it a competitor whose moat is content infrastructure rather than product superiority?

### 4. Authority

**Template:** `is [your brand] worth it`

**What to look for:** the answer's tone. The model is going to summarize the public sentiment it has retrieved. Good AI recommendation surfaces aggregate G2, Reddit, podcast transcripts, your own blog, and customer logos. If the answer is hesitant or generic, your public sentiment surface is thin.

**Rubric:** does the verdict cite specific reasons (use cases, pricing, support, integrations)? Or is it the generic "depends on your needs" non-answer?

### 5. Adjacent

**Template:** `alternatives to [top competitor]`

**What to look for:** this is the highest-intent query in the entire audit. Someone asking for alternatives is at the bottom of a competitor's funnel and you should be in the answer. If you're not, that's not a content problem — that's a positioning problem visible from the outside.

**Rubric:** are you in the top three alternatives? Is the framing of you accurate? Does the model give a reason to consider you, or just list you?

## How to grade

Score each (query × platform) cell from 0 to 4 using this rubric:

- **0 — Invisible.** Your brand does not appear at all.
- **1 — Named.** You're mentioned, no context, no positioning.
- **2 — Named with context.** You're mentioned with a use case or category descriptor.
- **3 — Named with comparison.** The model articulates *why* you, often relative to a competitor.
- **4 — Named as default.** You are the first or framed as the obvious recommendation.

Five queries × three platforms = 15 cells. Maximum score: 60. Sum, divide by 60, multiply by 100. That's your categorical citation share — a single number any CMO can defend in a board meeting, and that any head of marketing can move quarter over quarter.

A note on grading rigor: do this twice. Once cold, once after waiting 30 minutes. AI surfaces have non-trivial variance per session. If two grades differ by more than one point on a cell, mark it volatile — that volatility itself is signal that you don't yet have a stable position on that surface.

<aside>**Why this changes paid math.** When AI has pre-sold your category and named you as default, the visitor arriving at your landing page is a different visitor — already half-decided. Quality Score climbs because intent climbs; CPC drops because the auction stops being about your brand defending itself. Pre-decided traffic is the cheapest traffic you'll ever buy.</aside>

## The three things you'll see

When we run this for prospects, the result lands in one of three buckets almost every time.

**Bucket 1: your brand never appears.** This is the most common outcome, and the most actionable. It means you do not exist to the recommendation layer. The fix is not "more SEO content." The fix is structured, citable proof — pricing pages with named tiers, customer pages with named outcomes, comparison pages that handle competitor queries directly, and PR placements in the sources models actually retrieve. Two quarters of disciplined work moves the needle here, and the work compounds because models cache.

**Bucket 2: you appear but mis-positioned.** You're in the list, but the model has slotted you somewhere wrong — "X is the budget option" when you're the premium tier; "Y is for solo founders" when you sell to fifty-seat teams. This is more dangerous than invisibility because it's confidently wrong. The fix is corrective signal: rewrite your own primary surfaces to articulate the right positioning in plain sentences, then push that same framing into the third-party sources models retrieve. Reviews, podcast appearances, partner pages.

**Bucket 3: you appear with stale or wrong context.** Old pricing, an old feature list, a quote from an old funding round, a customer who's no longer a customer. Models retrieve and average; if half of your public surface is two years stale, you get a confidently outdated answer. The fix is mechanical: audit every public sentence about your brand and update or remove. Most brands have never done this once. Doing it once is a competitive advantage.
