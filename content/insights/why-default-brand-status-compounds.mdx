The first brand a model names for a category gets a feedback loop most marketers have never had access to. When ChatGPT cites you, users click through, write about it, tweet it, screenshot it. That secondary content becomes the next training and retrieval substrate. Six months later, the model isn't naming you because of your original positioning — it's naming you because users named you because the model named you. The flywheel is recursive, the moat is durable, and the first mover advantage is measured in quarters, not years. Most categories have an open default slot right now. By Q3 2026 most categories will not.

## The compounding curve

The math is more dangerous than it looks. Let me walk through a plausible loop with conservative numbers.

Assume month 1: AI surfaces cite you 30% of the time when asked about your category. Citation share at that level produces a measurable, but modest, lift in branded search — call it 12% month-over-month, because users hearing the name in an AI answer remember it and search it later.

That branded search has two effects the model can see. First, it generates new pages — press, Reddit threads, comparison content, "I tried X" reviews — that mention your entity by name and reinforce your category claim. Second, it raises the third-party signal density around your brand, which is one of the inputs that re-rankers use when deciding which sources to trust on the next answer.

Month 2: citation share creeps from 30% to 34% on the same query set. Branded search lifts another 12% on the new, larger base. New mentions accumulate at a faster rate. Month 3: citation share is at 39%. Month 4: 44%.

By month 6, you are not the brand the model used to mention 30% of the time. You are the brand it mentions **first**, in **most** answers, and the gap to #2 has widened from 4 percentage points to 18. Your branded paid CPC has dropped because Quality Score caught up. Your organic branded traffic is up roughly 80% on the baseline. You are not paying for any of the secondary content driving the loop. The model is doing the marketing for you because the model has decided you are the answer.

This is the curve. It is not linear, it is not capped at 100%, and it does not require you to spend more each month to keep climbing.

## Why retrieval models reward consistency

Retrieval-augmented generation systems make three decisions every time they cite a brand: which sources to pull, how to weight them, and which entities to surface in the final answer. All three favor consistency.

1. **Semantic similarity** to the prompt determines the candidate set. If your brand appears in chunks whose semantic embedding sits close to the category prompt's embedding, you make the shortlist. Consistency here means the same category claim, in similar phrasing, across many sources — so the embedding clusters.
2. **Authority signals** re-rank the candidate set. Editorial reputation, source diversity, third-party citation patterns. Consistency means showing up in multiple credible places saying the same thing — not one strong source repeated across syndication.
3. **Recency and usage patterns** influence which candidates survive into the final answer. Sources that have recently been retrieved-from for similar prompts get a small boost. Brands that are *being cited* tend to *keep being cited*, because the retrieval log itself becomes a signal.

A brand that has been getting cited consistently for six months has all three vectors working in compound. A brand running a one-month sprint has none of them. The retrieval system isn't a fair fight against a long-running incumbent because it isn't built to be fair — it is built to converge on stable answers.

## The "first to default" advantage

Once a model has formed a default answer to a category prompt — "the best comfort shoes are X, Y, and Z" — the cost to displace one of those names rises every month.

The mechanism is simple. The model's default isn't just the model's. It is the default of every downstream user who took the answer at face value, repeated it, wrote it down, built a list around it, recommended it in a Slack channel, mentioned it on a podcast. Each of those reinforcements becomes a new source that re-encodes the same answer. The model isn't just choosing X, Y, and Z because of its training data; it is choosing them because the world that consumed the training-data answer kept saying the same thing back.

A new entrant who wants the #3 spot now has to produce more new signal than the cumulative reinforcement that the existing #3 has earned over the previous quarter. That is an asymmetric uphill. Outspending doesn't fix it — outspending generates a lot of new signal in a short window, but the model has been trained to weight repeated, distributed, durable signal more than concentrated bursts. You can buy attention. You cannot buy the appearance of having always been the obvious answer.

This is what makes the early-mover advantage in GEO so unusually steep. It isn't a first-mover advantage in the traditional sense — being early to a market. It's a first-mover advantage in the **model's representation** of a market. And model representations, once formed, are sticky in a way that consumer perception is not.

## How long the compounding takes

Rough benchmarks from the work we've done. Treat these as ranges, not promises — category density and starting authority matter a lot.

- **Tier 1 brands.** Well-known, lots of editorial coverage, established Wikipedia presence, durable third-party signal. From a standing start (not currently dominant in AI), they typically reach dominant citation share in **60-90 days** of consistent GEO work. The baseline material is there; the work is mostly coherence, schema, and per-platform tuning.
- **Tier 2 brands.** Mid-market, some PR, a presence on review platforms, partial Wikipedia presence or none. **120-180 days** to dominant. The work is meaningfully larger: producing or earning new third-party coverage, building entity records, fixing inconsistencies, plus the tuning work.
- **Tier 3 brands.** Challengers, recently-launched companies, sparse coverage. **180-365 days** to dominant. The work is foundational: establishing the entity at all, earning the editorial coverage that a Tier 1 brand started with for free, then layering the GEO craft on top.

The compounding behavior is similar in all three tiers. The starting point is what shifts. A Tier 3 brand that starts now and a Tier 1 brand that starts in eight months will reach citation parity at roughly the same time — but only if both are running consistent programs.

<aside>
**What happens when the default is wrong.** Occasionally a brand becomes the default for the wrong reason. A viral post, a misattributed quote, a comparison piece that named them as the top choice when they were really the third choice. The model picks it up, the loop kicks in, and within a quarter they are the default answer for a category they aren't really the best in. The wrong default holds for a remarkably long time — typically two to four quarters — because the loop reinforces the answer regardless of whether the answer is correct. Two things eventually break it. One is a model refresh that re-weights the source corpus and surfaces a more accurate picture. The other is an editorial event large enough to overwhelm the existing signal. Until then, the wrong default behaves exactly like the right default. The model does not have an opinion about whether you deserve the spot. It just has the spot.
</aside>

## How defaults break

Defaults are durable but not permanent. There are three things that break them, and operators on both sides of the equation — defenders and challengers — should understand all three.

1. **A major model refresh.** When a foundation model is re-trained on a new corpus, the previous answer can shift. Not always, but sometimes. We saw GPT-4 defaults that didn't survive into GPT-4-turbo, and again into the 4o generation. If you are the default, treat every model release as a soft risk and re-baseline within two weeks. If you are the challenger, model releases are your most reliable opening.
2. **An editorial event.** A Forbes piece, a TechCrunch list, a viral X thread from a credible voice — any high-authority source that crisply names a competitor as the best in your category becomes a single high-weight signal that the model can latch onto. Defenders pre-empt these with their own editorial program. Challengers earn one, then immediately stack supporting coverage in the next 30 days to make it stick.
3. **A category redefinition.** Someone coins a new term, the term takes off, and now the prompts users actually type don't match the category you were dominant in. You were the leader in `"workflow automation"`. The term shifted to `"AI agents"`. The model's old defaults for the old term don't carry. This is the most expensive kind of disruption and the hardest to see coming.

If one of these happens to you and you are the incumbent, the playbook is immediate: re-baseline within 14 days, re-audit per platform, ship a fresh wave of structured content that reasserts your category claim, and pursue at least one new editorial placement that re-anchors the entity. Don't wait for the loss to compound. The same flywheel that built your position will erode it just as quickly if it gets pointed the other way.

## The window is right now

The reason this matters in 2026 and not in 2028 is simple. The vast majority of categories — our internal estimate is 99% — have not yet formed durable AI defaults. The models will name a few brands when asked, but the answers vary across sessions, drift between platforms, and respond easily to new signal.

That is what an open category looks like. It does not stay open for long.

The brands that move now will set the defaults in their category. The brands that wait until they see competitors getting cited will be running uphill against a flywheel they let someone else start. The window in most categories is months, not years. Some — solar, B2B SaaS verticals, mid-market e-commerce — have a window measured in weeks.
